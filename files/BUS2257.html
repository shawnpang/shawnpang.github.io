1.2 Adjusting Entries

  • Purpose
    • ensure that revenues are recorded in the period earned and expenses are recorded in the period incurred.
  • When to do
    • Prepared at the end of the period
    • It would be too time consuming to record adjusting entries every day
  • How to do
    • Deferred Revenue
      • Example: unearned revenue
      • Adjusting Entry

        DR Unearned Revenue
            CR Revenue

    • Deferred Expense

      • Example: Depreciation/Amortization, Prepaid Expenses
      • Adjusting Entry
        DR Expense
             CR Asset
    • Accrued Revenue
      • Example: Interest Receivable
      • Adjusting Entry
        DR Expense
            CR Asset
    • Accrued Expense
      • Example: Interest Payable
      • Adjusting Entry
        DR Expense
             CR Liability

1.3 Closing Entries

  • Purpose
    • To transfer net income/loss to Reatined Earnings
    • To establish a zero balance in each of the temporary accounts
  • Permanent vs. Temporary Accounts
    • Permanent Accounts
      • used in Statement of Financial Position
      • accounts whose balances are carried into the next accounting period (assets, liabilities & equity)
    • Temporary Accounts
      • used in Income Statement
      • accounts that are closed to the Retained Earnings account
      • Income Summary is a special temporary account used to close the revenue and expense accounts
  • Steps
    • Revenue
      • Debit revenue accounts with credit balances, Credit Income Summary
      • Credit revenue accounts with debit balances, Debit Income Summary
    • Expense
      • Credit expense accounts with debit balances, Debit Income Summary
      • Debit expense accounts with credit balances, Credit Income Summary
    • Income Tax
      • Subtotal Income Summary, calculate and record tax.
      • Income Tax Expense, Credit Income Tax Payable.
    • Net Income After Tax
      • Credit Income Tax Expense, Debit Income Summary
    • Close Income Summary to Retained Earnings

2. Bank Reconciliations

2.1 Introduction

  • Purpose
    • Verify that the company and bank records are correct and in agreement.
    • Determine the proper cash balance that should appear on the company's statement of financial position.
    • Prepare correcting entries to update company records to reflect the proper cash balance.
  • Notes
    • Unless otherwise stated, assume discrepancies between the bank’s and the company’s records were the company’s errors.
    • Also, assume the company made the entry for the same value as listed on either receipts or disbursements

2.2 How to Do Reconciliation

  • Balance Per Bank's Records
    1. Openning balance taken from E/B on bank statement
    2. Adjust the bank's balance for any items the bank did not know about:
      • Add all outstanding deposits (deposits in transit at the time statement)
      • Subtract all outstanding cheques (uncashed cheques)
      • check for bank errors (rare but possible) and add/subtract accordingly.
    3. New adjusted bank balance should reconcile with adjusted depositor's balance
  • Balance Per Depositor's Records
    1. Openning balance (taken from last period's adjusted balance on bank reconciliation or last period's statement of financial position)
    2. Add cash receipts less cash disbursements
    3. Adjust company balance for any items the bank added or subtracted which we did not know about:
      • Receivables/notes collected for company by bank
      • Service charges and NSF cheques
      • Automatic Payments (loans, interest, payroll, etc.)
      • Add or subtract from balance accordingly to get new adjusted balance
      • check for errors in recording transactions

2.3 Suggested Process

  1. Go to last month's bank reconciliation
    • Check to make sure that any late deposits or outstanding cheques are listed in this month's bank statement.
    • Circle those that are not.
    • Check off these items on the bank statement
  2. Go to the cash disbursements list (cheques register)
    • Check off each cheque as it appears in this month's bank statement.
    • Circle any errors that appear.
    • Circle cheques that do not appear on the bank statement
  3. Go to the cash receipts list (bank deposits)
    • Check off each deposit as it appears in this month's bank statement. Circle any errors that appear.
    • Circle deposits that do not appear on the bank statement.
  4. Go to the bank statement
    • Circle items that are not checked off.

3. Petty Cash

3.1 What is Petty Cash

  • Petty cash is an asset account set up by companies to handle low value transactions
  • The employee in charge of petty cash will hold a small amount of money and will reimburse others for minor costs
  • Once the funds run low, the petty cash holder will submit receipts to the company's accountant to get the funds replenished.

3.2 Petty Cash Transactions

  • To establish a petty cash account

    DR Petty Cash (asset)
         CR Cash

    DR Various Expenses

  • Note: once the petty cash is established, no other accounting transactions run through the Petty Cash account unless the fund's level is increased or decreased.

4. Accounts Receivable

4.1 Accounts Receivable

  • Definitions
    • Represents money owed to a company by its customers for goods and services purchased with credit
    • Considered a trade account, as a current asset on the balance sheet
  • Transaction Entry
    DR Accounts Receivable
        CR Sales Revenue

4.2 Allowance for Doubtful Accounts

  • Purpose
    • Customers may not always pay the amount in the Accounts Receivable on time (late or never).
    • There is a valid business cost associated with offering trade credit.
    • Signal to the users of our statements the dollar value of credit accounts we expect we will be unable to collect.
    • By using a contra asset account called Allowance for Doubtful Accounts we are able to lower the value of our accounts receivable to represent what we expect to be able to collect.
  • Introduction
    • Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (A4DA) has a normal credit balance as it is a contra asset, to lower the value of accounts receivable.
    • It is an estimation of the accounts that will not be collectable during the period.
    • Combined with the accounts receiable balance, A4DA shows the net realizable value of accounts receivable as they would appear on the balance sheet:

4.3 Writing off a Bad Account

  • Introduction
    • Write-off taking an account out of accounts receivable because it will not be paid.
    • A write-off will lower the balance in A4DA as a way of using a portion of the allowance we had for uncollectable accounts.
  • Entry
    DR Allowance for Doubtful Accounts
        CR Accounts Receivable

4.4 Recovering an Account Previously Written Off

  • Purpose
    • Sometimes a company will write-off an account expecting not to receive payment, and then will unexpectedly receive payment for the previously written-off account.
    • When this situation occurs, we must recover the previously written-off account and re-instate the account as an accounts receivable.

  • Entries

    • Record the Recovery

      DR Accounts Receivable
          CR Allowance for Doubtful Accounts

    • Record the Collection

      DR Cash
          CR Accounts Receivable

4.5 Estimating Bad Debt Expense

  • Purpose
    • At the end of each period, one adjusting entry is made to Bad Debt Expense (DR) and Allowance for Doubtful Accounts (CR)
    • The bad debt expense represents:
      • Accounts that have gone bad from this period's sales.
      • Accounts that might go bad from this period's sales.
    • Accounts are seldom written off in the same period in which the sale was made (expense will not match revenue).
      • therefore we must estimate the number or amount of accounts that will be written off in subsequent periods, and record the expense in this period.
  • Estimation
    • Aging of Accounts Receivable Method
      • The aging method takes place by sorting a company's accounts receivable according to the dates of these unpaid invoices.
      • The aging will be reviewed in order to determine the approximate amount of the receivables that may not be collected.
    • Percentage of Accounts Receivable Method
      • The company's management determines, on average, what proportion, or percentage, of outstanding accounts receivable are typically uncollectable.
      • This value then becomes the ending balance in allowance for doubtful accounts.
  • Steps
    1. Calculate Trial Balance in A4DA
    2. Calculate Ending Balance in A4DA with either method
    3. Calculate Bad Debt Expense
    4. Record
  • Entry
    DR Bad Debt Expense
        CR A4DA

  • You must credit the inventory account directly for the two entries.

7.2 Entries for Purchase Discounts and Return/Allowance

  • Purchase Discounts

    • Quantity Discount is given usually when the buyer buys a large quantity of goods, and is not accounted for specially (it just changes the price).
    • Payment Discount is given when the customer pays in cash or pays early, and is accounted separately.

      DR Accounts Payable
          CR Inventory

  • Purchase return/allowance

    DR Accounts Payable / Cash
        CR Inventory

7.3 Key Points

  1. Record all relevant costs in the Inventory account.
    • Purchases, duties and transportation-in are debits.
    • Purchase discounts and returns and allowances are credits.
  2. Ignore “net cost”
    • Only record discounts when the liability is paid within the discount period.
  3. Calculate COGS at the end of the period only
    • We only use the periodic inventory,
  4. Record transportation charges, incurred when delivering goods to
    a customer, in an expense account

8.2 Suggested Approach

  1. Read the case carefully. Highlight important items such as duties, FOB terms, credit
    terms, etc.
  2. Go to case exhibits. Understand what is contained in them.
  3. Handle each purchase individually and in sequence of when ownership took place.
    • Break out all of the relevant costs individually.
    • Keep track of the net units per purchase in its own T-account
    • Calculate the unit cost of the purchase.
  4. Determine the cost of goods available for sale (COGAFS) and units available for sale (UAFS).
  5. Calculate the value of the ending inventory using one of the three methods available:
    • Specific Identification
    • First-in, first-out (FIFO)
    • Average cost

8.3 Rounding Rules

  • Place all calculations in the Inventory () Ending Balance and COGS).
  • Round all unit costs to three decimal places.

8.4 Ending Inventory Valuation

  • Specific Identification
    • Specific identification is a good inventory valuation method to use if the company carries high-price, low-volume inventory, must be used to cost inventories that are not ordinarily interchangeable.
    • Purchase inventory records must be used to assign actual cost to units in ending balance.
  • First-in, first-out (FIFO)
    • The cost flow assumption assumes that the oldest inventory is "sold" first.
    • The ending balance of inventory is valued at the most recent purchase price(s).
    • • FIFO produces a more "relevant" balance sheet since the E/B in inventory reflects its current value.
  • Average cost
    • The average cost inventory valuation method is well suited to companies that carry homogeneous inventory.
    • The advantage to using this inventory valuation method is that there is no way to manipulate income.
    • Computed by dividing total cost of goods available for sale (COGAFS) by total units available for sale (UAFS), then multiplying by the ending balance (units).

8.5 LCNRV Rule

  • Net Realizable Value is value the inventory at the estimated selling price less any variable selling costs.
  • Rule
    • Inventory should not be carried at a value greater than the amount expected to be realized from its sale.
    • When the net realizable value of inventory is lower than its cost, inventory is written down to its net realizable value.
  • Steps
    1. Determine the cost of the inventory.
    2. Determine the net realizable value of the inventory
    3. Compare the cost valuation to the net realizable value
    4. Record the inventory at the lower of the cost or net realizable value.

9.2 Sales, Retirements and Trade-ins of Long Lived Assets

  1. Depreciate the asset up to the point of sale, retirement or trade-in.
  2. Calculate the asset's book value at the point of the sale, retirement, or trade-in
  3. Compare the asset's book value with the cash received or the trade-in allowance gtanted.
    • If the BV is greater than the cash received or the trade-in allowance granted, then a LOSS has occurred. Losses are recorded as EXPENSES.
    • If the BV is less than the cash received or the trade-in allowance granted, then a GAIN has occurred. Gains are recorded as a reduction in EXPENSES (contra-expense account).
  4. Record the entry
    • Record the effect on cash
      • In the case of a sale - Debit
      • In the case of a trade-in - Credit
    • Remove the old asset from the accounting records
      • Historical Cost
      • Accumulated Depreciation
    • Record the gain/loss
    • In In the case of a trade-in, add the new asset to the accounting records.

9.4 Intangible Assets

  • Introduction
    • Intangible assets do not have physical substance, and involve rights, privileges, and/or competitive advantages.
    • Intangibles include assets such as goodwill, patents, trademarks etc.
    • There are 2 types of intangible assets: ones that have finite life, and ones that have indefinite life.
      • Finite Lives: patents, copyrights, research and development costs
      • Indefinite Loves: trademarks, brands, franchises and licenses
  • Accounting for Intangible Assets
    • Intangible Assets with Finite Life
      • Recorded at cost.
      • For an intangible asset with a finite life, its cost must be allocated over its useful life. (This is called amortization.)
        • The amortizable amount would be amortized equally over the length of useful life selected.
        • Amortizable amount = cost less residual value
        • Useful life = the shorter of 1) the estimated useful life and 2) the legal life
    • Intangible Assets with Indefinite life
      • Recorded at cost.
      • For an asset with an indefinite life, we must consider what we call impairment, instead of amortization.
      • If any impairment is evident, it must be written down to its recoverable amount.

9.5 Research & Development Costs

  • Research and development is considered when a company develops intangible assets internally rather than acquiring them from another party.
  • During research, costs are incurred but it is not yet known if these costs will have a future benefit.
    • These costs are recorded in the asset account "Development Costs"
  • Once certain criteria is met, expenditures are considered to be development with a known future benefit.
    • These costs are recorded in the asset account “Development Costs”.
    • Criteria for “Development Costs”
      • The project is technically feasible
      • The company has the desire to complete the development
      • The company is able to complete the development
      • A market exists for the product

10.2 Raw Materials

  • Increase in Balance
    • The Raw Materials account is treated in the same way as the retail inventory accounts we have dealt with thus far.
  • Ending Balance
    • Calculated with one of the three methods
      • specific identification
      • weighted average cost
      • FIFO
    • equals Cost of Raw Materials Available for Use
  • Decrease in Balance (Plug)
    • The only difference is that the PLUG number (formerly COGS) is now the cost of raw materials used (CORMU) and should be debited to the WIP account.

10.3 Work-in-Process (WIP)

  • Increase in Balance
    • The Work in Process (WIP) account includes all PRODUCT costs, which fall under one of three categories:
      • Raw Materials
      • Direct Labour
      • Factory Overhead
  • Ending Balance
    • Formula
      • the formula is
      • pRM = partial Raw Materials
      • pDL = partial Direct Labour
      • pFOH = partial Factory Overhead
    • Calculation of pRM
      • Raw materials can be traced to individual units.
      • A company’s production workers or engineers can estimate how much raw material has been used in each unit and then calculate the cost of raw materials in the partially completed goods inventory.
    • Calculation of pDL
      • Job timing sheets or standard times can be used to get a reasonable estimate as to how much time has been put into the partially completed goods.
      • This figure would be multiplied by an average direct labour cost to get pDL
    • Calculation of pFOH
      • These costs are more difficult to assign, and therefore we must make an estimate to value pFOH
      • In order to allocate factory overhead, managers look for a cost to serve as a “proxy", like machine hours, direct labour, or materials that are
        • Measurable in the partially completed units
        • Considered to be a cost driver (i.e., an item that tends to be somewhat proportional to overhead costs)
        • Spread evenly throughout the production process
  • Decrease in Balance (Plug)
    • Cost of Finished Goods Manufactured

10.4 Finished Goods

  • Although there are two methods for valuing finished goods inventory, for the purposes of this course, the ending balance will be calculated using the weighted average cost method

11. Leases

11.1 Introduction to Leases

  • Leasor & Leasee
    • The lessor is the party providing the asset
    • The lessee is the party making payments for the use of the asset
  • Classification of Leases
    • Operating Lease
      • A lease is classified as an operating lease under IFRS if the lease term is for a period of one year or less and does not contain a purchase option or the leased asset has a low value when new.
    • Finance Lease
      • A lease is classified as a finance lease under IFRS if the lease term is for a period longer than one year, or the lease contains a purchase option.

11.2 Accounting for Operating Leases

13. Bonds

13.1 Issuance

  • Note:
    • Record the carrying value (book value of the bonds in a Bonds Payable account)
    • If the market price of the bonds is not give, (i.e. issued at 97), but a market interest rate is available, use present values to determine the issue/market price.

13.2 Interest and Bond Amortization

  • Note:
    • Interest and amortization are accounted for using the effective interest rate method.
      • When using this method, interest and amortization are calculated at each interest payment date, major transaction dates, and at fiscal year-end.
      • Since the book value of the bond changes each period, the amortization will also be different.
    • Calculating interest and amortization involves three steps:
      • Total Interest Expense

        BV refers to the book value of the bond on the date of issuance or the last interest payment date; Market Interest Rate refers to the market interest rate on the date of issuance.
    • Interest Payment (paid or accrued)
    • Bond Amortization

  • Transactions

    • If interest is paid

      DR/CR Bonds Payable
      DR Bond Interest Expense
          CR Cash

    • If interest is accrued

      DR/CR Bonds Payable
      DR Bond Interest Expense
          CR Bond Interest Payable

13.3 Early Retirement of Bonds

  • Steps
    1. Record interest and amortize the bonds being retired up to the retirement date.
    2. Determine the cash that needs to be paid to bondholders for having their bonds called (CR to cash)
    3. Compare the redemption price to the bond carrying value to determine the gain or loss on retirement.
    4. Write off carrying value of retired bonds (DR to Bonds Payable), and record gain or loss.

  • Entries

    • Entry for a Loss on Redemption

      DR Bonds Payable
      DR Loss on Redemption
          CR Cash

    • Entry on a Gain on Redemption

      DR Bonds Payable
          CR Gain on Redemption
          CR Cash

14.5 Year-end Valuation

  • Notes
    • A company must adjust the book value of its trading investments at fiscal year-end to match the securities market value, and the processes for stocks and bonds are very similar.
  • Steos
    1. Step 1: Calculate market value
      • Stocks - use selling price per stock
      • Bonds - there are two possibilities
        1) Face value by the market rate (e.g. a 1,03).
        2) Use current market interest rates to present value both the face value and interest payments remaining on the bond and add them together.
    2. Step 2: Determine the book value of the trading investments by referring to the relevant balance in your trading investment account.
    3. Step 3: Calculate and record unrealized gains or losses.
      • To calculate unrealized gains and losses, subtract the cost of the market security from its market value.
      • If the market value is higher than cost, record an unrealized gain in the revenue section and debit the marketable security;
      • If it's lower than cost, record an unrealized loss in the expense section and credit the marketable security.

15. Comprehensive Accounting Cases

15.1 Case Framework

  • Recommended steps of doing the comprehensive case are the following:
    1. Read the case
    2. Post openning balances to T-accounts (check last year's balance sheet)
    3. Post cash disbursements and cash receipts to the appropriate T-accounts.
      • Do not attempt to adjust or allocate these costs at this time.
    4. Post accounts payable lists.
    5. Go through the case paragraph by paragraph in the order given, recording transactions and/or making adjustments when necessary.
      • Do not re-record cash entries already posted from step 3.
    6. Make any remaining end-of-period adjusting entries
    7. Record closing entries.
    8. Prepare financial statements if required.

15.2 Disbursement and Receipts

  • Cash disbursements are a list of all cash expenditures made throughout the year.
  • Cash receipts are a list of collections of cash.
  • The purpose of recording cash disbursements and cash receipts is to account for all of the cash spent or received throughout the year.
    • Thus, once you have recorded disbursements and receipts, it is unlikely you will need to make many (if any) additional entries to the Cash account for the rest of the case.
    • Also, do not try to allocate or adjust these cash disbursements and cash receipts at this time, because here the purpose is only about cash.

15.3 Posting Accounts Payable

  • Two lists of Accounts Payable
    • One will be a list of outstanding accounts payable as of the end of the last year.
      • The sum of these outstanding payables equal the ending balance of the accounts payable as found on last year's balance sheet, and therefore also your openning balance.
    • The second list relates to those payables still outstanding at the end of the current fiscal year.

  • Adjusting Entries

    • Last Year's List

      • Rationale
        • If cash disbursements were not given, you would debit Accounts Payable and credit Cash to account for the payment of the outstanding Accounts Payable.
        • However, when making the cash disbursement, you made a debit to the expense account or to the asset account, and a credit to Cash.
        • Therefore, we need to correct the previos entries
      • Correcting Entries

        DR Accounts Payable
            CR Expense / Asset

    • This Year's List

      • Rationale
        • The accounts payable list indicate that some expenditures were made this year, but not yet paid for in cash.
        • These transactions have not yet been recorded since they were not part of the cash disbursements or cash receipts.
      • Correcting Entries

        DR Expense / Asset
            CR Accounts Payable

15.4 Proceeding Through the Case Paragraph by Paragraph

  • What to check when reading
    • The cash disbursements were recorded in the correct account.
    • You have recorded the necessary adjusting entries to ensure that the entire transaction is correct.
    • All accrued adjusting entries (such as interest payable and amortization) will still need to be completed.
  • What to check when recording
    • Try to use a timeline to help you to clarify all the transactions
    • Even though a portion of the transaction may have been recorded already, you still must ensure that for all transactions, total debits equal total credits.

15.5 Closing Entries

  • Steps
    1. Close all revenues to the Income Summary account.
    2. Close the Cost of Goods Sold account and all operating expenses to the Income Summary account.
    3. Tally the Income Summary to determine the net income (loss) before tax.
    4. If a net income was earned, calculate th eincome tax.
    5. Close the Income Tax account to the Income Summary account.
    6. Close the Net Income After Tax from the Income Summary to Retained Earnings account.
  • Tax related
    • The income tax incurred during the year is determined by the tax rate by multiplying by the income earned.
      • If a net loss was incurred, do not calculate the income tax.
    • When posting the cash disbursements, you may have noticed an income tax payment.
      • If the company has an Income Tax Payable account, it is wise to debit the cash disbursement to that account.
      • The debit will often exceed the opening balance in the Income Tax Payable account. This is because the company has paid all of last year's taxes payable and prepaid some taxes for this fiscal year.

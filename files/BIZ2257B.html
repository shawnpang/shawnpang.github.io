1. Cost Behaviour

1.1 Cost Classification

  • What is a Cost
    • Cost: goods or services used to generate revenue, is a revurring flow
    • Investment benefits many periods, and is a one-time flow
  • Cost Classification: Product/Period, Fixed/Variable
Classification Cost of Goods Sold (Product Costs) Operating Expense(Period Cost)
Fixed Costs e.g., supervisor's salary e.g., executive salary
Variable Costs e.g., direct labour e.g., sales commissions

  • Classification Definitions: Fixed, Variable Semi-Variable Step

    • Fixed Costs: do not change with changes in activity level, like rent, supervisor salary, and interest on a loan.
    • Variable Costs: change directly with variable in activity level, like raw materials and direct labor.
    • Semi-variable Costs: have a portion of the cost changes directly with variables in activity level, combining fixed and variable cost.
      • Usually semi-variable cost items have a base cost to cover a certain range of uses, and then start charging on a per unit basis: like utilities or phone plan.
    • Step Costs: do not change in direct proportion with changes in volume, but do change in large amounts at distinct volumes, can be viewed as fixed costs within a narrow relevant range.
      • When you need to incur a step cost, that's when your activity level has risen to a completely higher level, like when the production increases too much so a second supervisor is needed for night shift.

1.4 Risk Assessments Tools

  • Margin of Safety:

    • By how much can projections be off before the venture becomes a breakeven proposition?

  • Breakeven as a Percentage of Market

    • How much of the current must be captured in order to breakeven?
    • However, the interpretation can vary depending on the definition of the market, and the market characteristics

  • ROI

    • Measured as a percentage and must be compared to a hurdle tate that is specific to the company, the industry, and the venture.
    • The more risky the venture, the higher the hurdle rate. And the higher the ROI, the more enticing the venture.

  • Payback

    • Measured as the # of years needed for the return to pay back the investment.
    • This is the reciprocal of ROI.

3. Cash Budgeting

3.1 Cash Budget

  • What is Cash Budget
    • A forecasting tool that tracks all cash receipts and cash disbursements
    • Done on a shorter time frame than other statements (i.e., month-by-month or even week-by-week)
  • Why is Cash Budget Important
    • Allows companies to predict possible cash shortages and take corrective actions before a crisis occurs
    • Allows companies to see if large sums of excess cash are lying idle as they could be put to better use
  • Comparison with Accrural Accounting
    • Instead of the matchign principle where we match expenses with revenues in the period in which they are incurred, now we are concerned with matching cash inflows and cash outflows in the period in which they are occurred.
    • All cash items, regardless of their classification, are accounted for in a cash budget.
    • Non-cash items never appear.
  • Comparison Table
Item Effect on Cash Effect on Profit
Fixed Assets Payment terms: likely year of purchase Depreciate over useful life
Credit Sales "Age" of accounts receivable Revenue recognized when service earned
Purchase on credit supplier repayment policy match with revenues
Intangibles Payment terms Amortize over useful life

3.2 Methodology of the Cash Budget

  1. Identify the nature and timing of all cash inflows.

    • Cash Inflow Items
      • inflows from financing - usually a one-time basis
      • investment income collection - sporadic
      • collections from sales - recurring over time
    • Tools to Forecase Sales
      • Management's estimates
      • Historial sales patterns
      • Industry trends
      • Competitor's sales

  2. Identify the nature and timing of all cash outflows.

    • Cash Outflow Items
      • cash operating expenditures, like rent, supplies, inventory.
      • capital expenditures, like purchase of equipment
      • financial commitments, like interet/principle payment
      • equity reduction, like drawings, dividends
  3. Subtract outflows from inflows to get net cash flow for the period (either a surplus or a deficit)
  4. Add/subtract cash flow for the period to the ending balance in cash from the previous period to get new ending balance

3.3 Uses of Cash Budgeting

  • Schedule Timing of Cash Flow
    • make efficient use of cash
    • analyze solvency
    • forecast financial requirements
    • prioritize and plan payments of outstanding accounts
    • perform sensitivity to plan contingency action
    • categorize type of financing requirements
  • Important Implications
    • Always need to do fine tuning constantly
    • Cash budgeting is not perfect

3.4 Cash Analysis

  • What is the maximum cash requirements?
    • In what month is it needed?
    • Can we secure this much financing?
  • Is the overall cash flow positive or negative?
    • If it is negative, what can be done?
  • Which variables have the biggest impact on cash flow?
    • What happens if these variables change (sensitivity)?
  • If negative cash flow results, what is the main cause?
    • Is it a short-term or a long-term problem?
    • What is the best type of financing to alleviate the problem?

4. Statement of Cash Flows

4.1 Introduction

  • Purpose
    • To provide information as to how the activities of the enterprise have been financed and how its financial esources have been used during the period covered by the statement.
    • Answer the questions
      • where did our money come from?
      • where did we use it?
  • End Goal
    • dissect the cash account
    • reveal the combination of factors causing the changes to the cash account
    • understand what this means to the company

4.2 How to Make a Statement of Cash Flow

  • Operating Activities
    • How much cash did we generate (use) from our regular day-to-day operations?
    • Steps to get the Net Cash Flow from Operations
      1. Record net income
      2. Add back all non-cash expenses.
      3. Negate the effects of items which do not relate to normal business activities such as the loss on sale of a fixed asset. Add back loss, subtract gains.
      4. Account for the changes in current accounts associated with normal operations.
        • A/An increase (decrease) of a current asset is a use (source) of cash.
        • A/An increase (decrease) of a current liability is a source (use) of cash.
  • Financing Activities
    • How much cash was generated through financing activities (debt or equity financing)?
    • Steps to get the Net Sources of Cash from Financing
      1. Look at the statement of financial position for any increase or decrease in the level or loans, stock, personal investment.
      2. Reconstruct the Retained Earlings Account (or Owner's Equity Account) to determine the level of dividends (or drawings).
      3. Subtract dividends/drawings.
  • Investng Activities
    • How much cash was used (generated) from investments (or divestments).
    • Steps
      1. Focus on the Statement of Financial Positions and consider the effects on cash of all the changes in the level of investment for non-current assets.
      2. Reconstruct any depreciable asset accounts considering depreciation and any losses or gains on disposals in order to determine the cash impact of fixed asset transactions.
  • Calculating Ending Cash
    • Did the company have a positive or negative cash flow what was the impact on the company's ending cash balance?
    • Steps:
      1. Add net cash flow from operations, cash flow from financing and cash flow from investments to the opening cash balance for the year - i.e., last year's ending balance from the SOFP
      2. This total should reconcile with the ending cash balance for this fiscal year.

4.3 Special Cases

  • Reasoning
    • Retained Earnings and Fixed Assets accounts must be reconstructed as they are a combinations of other amounts.
    • Retained Earnings -> Divident Payments
    • Net Fixed Assets -> Net Purchases/Disposals

4.4 Uses of the Statement of Cash Flow

  • Questions to Analyse
    • How much cash did we make/lose from operations? Is it sustainable?
    • What are the largest sources and uses of cash? Does this meet expectations? Is this sustainable?
    • Is this company appropriately matching its source/uses of cash?
  • Reminder when Analyzing
    • The interpretation must be environmentally specific and business specific.
    • The analysis should be used in conjunction with ratio analysis in order to answer the questions posed.
    • A business that is unprofitable can survive, but insolvency (insufficient cash to pay debts) could mean business failure.
  • Analysis Structure
    • Large Sources/Uses
    • Financing Matching
    • Whether and how additional financing is needed
    • Assets Purchases

5. Financial Ratio Analysis

5.1 Introduction

  • Uses of Ratios
    • External: investors, bankers, and analysts
    • Internal: key step in a financial size-up, aid in operations and financing decisions
  • Why Ratios
    • Compare on a base case instead of an absolute number.
  • Limitations of Ratios
    • Quantitative only
    • Standards of comparison is not perfect
    • Difficult to compare with other firms
    • Inflations may lead to misleading results
    • Bias during rapid growth stage or industry decline
    • Tell us what has happened, not what will happen.

6. Financial Alternatives and Projected Statements

6.1 Introduction

  • Why Use Projected Statements
    • Forcast cash needs
    • Provide information for creditors
    • View the future in dollars and perhaps alter operation policies so that goals may be reached
    • make the budget
  • Information Sources
    • Historical financial performance - ratios, old statements
    • Management policies and estimates
    • Environmental factors
    • Management's forecast

6.2 Methodology of Projecting Statements

  • Projected Income Statement
    1. Estimate sales forecast
    2. estimate expense levels using vertical analysis, management policy, and past income statements
    3. Apply tax rate

  • Projected Statement of Financial Positions

    1. Enter items not expected to change from the last statement of financial position.

    2. Enter owner's equity (retained earnings):

    3. Use liquidity analysis and management policy to determine expected level of accounts receivable, accounts payable, and inventory.

      • Projected Accounts Receivable

      • Projected Inventory

      • Projected Accounts Payable

    4. Include planned fixed asset acquisition and include depreciation from income statement.

    5. Include planned debt financing.
    6. Estimate accounts not yet filled using historical data.
    7. Use the cash budget to get cash and/or bank loan balances
      • If the subtotal of liabilities and equity exceeds the asset subtotal, plug for cash as no financing is needed.
      • If the asset subtotal exceeds the liabilities & equity subtotal, plug for additional financing or bank loan as more financing is needed.

6.3 Generic Financing Strategies

  • If, according to the projected statement of financial position, additional financing is required, then we should consider the financing options available.
  • Options
    • Internal Financing
      • Improve spread between cash revenues and cash expenses
      • Internal financing/working capital manipulation: lower receivables and inventory, and increase payables
      • Divest fixed assets by selling the not necessary ones
    • External Financing
      • Raise equity
      • Take on short-term or long-term debt
  • What to consider when choosing
    • The timing of cash needs (short-term or long-term)
    • The current financial structure
    • The likelihood of obtaining the financing

6.4 Sensitivity Analysis

  • Introduction to Sensitivity Analysis
    • Sensitivity is the art of playing what if.
    • Ask what happens if sales decrease/increase, raw materials increase, etc.
    • Because estimates are used, they will inevitably be wrong.
  • What Scenarios to Choose for Sensitivity Analysis
    • It is impossible to do a sensitivity analysis on every possible scenario, so we need to choose among all options.
    • Guidelines
      • The greater the uncertainty, the greater the chance of a wrong assumption.
      • The greater the impact on the bottom line, the more important it is to know what if.
    • Most Common Items for the Analysis
      • Sales
      • Cost of Goods Sold

7. Costing Methods

7.1 Cost/Price Relationship

  • As the goal of businesses is to make money, the price must exceed cost.
  • However, there are several defeinitions of cost, and we should decide which definition to use when setting prices:
    • Direct Cost
    • Absorption Cost
    • Full Cost
  • Choose the cost definition that best suits the problem at hand by considering
    • Consumers' willingness to pay
    • Competitive prices
    • Industry conditions
  • Also, remember that intelligent pricing decisions can only be made after a study of both internal costs and external demand.

7.2 Direct Cost

  • Direct cost per unit includes only the direct and traceable costs:
    • Direct material
    • Direct labour
    • Direct selling expenses
  • For all intents and purposes, direct cost/unit = variable cost/unit.
    • Therefore direct cost per unit does not change with changes in volume.
  • Uses of Direct Cost per Unit
    • Setting a floor price
    • Contribution analysis
    • Breakeven calculations

7.3 Absorption Cost

  • Absorption cost per unit includes all production costs regardless of their classification as fixed or variable.
    • Direct Materials
    • Direct Labour
    • Factory overhead

  • Can be calculated using the financial statements of manfacturing companies:

  • Absorption cost per unit changes depending on volume.

  • Uses of Absorption Cost per Unit
    • Comparing relative plant efficiency
    • Financial reporting

7.4 Full Cost

  • Full cost per unit include all cost in the price per unit.
    • Direct materials
    • Direct labour
    • All factory overhead
    • All selling expenses
    • All general and administrative charges
    • All financing charges
  • May need to make use of allocation rates to calculate full cost on a per unit basis.
  • Full cost per unit changes depending on volume.
  • Uses of Full Cost Per Unit
    • Long-term pricing strategy / determining normal price
    • Defence and cost-plus contracts.

9. Differential Analysis

9.1 Introduction to Differential Analysis

  • Differential Analysis
    • The differential cash flow model is a shor-term decision making framework used to assess future opportunities in a clear, concise manner.
    • The framework can only be used when analyzing two (or more) quantifiable, short-term alternatives. Many times one alternative is the status quo.
  • Decisions Solvable with Differential Analysis
    • Make in-house vs. buy
    • Accept vs. reject special order request
    • Lower vs. raise price
    • Maintain vs. delete sub-unit
    • Expand vs. status-quo
  • Why Differential Analysis
    • Preffered to Projected Statements because:
      • Projected statements are crowded with irrelevant information
      • Projected statements are accrual in nature, whereas the important decisions revolve around cash.
    • Preferred to Breakeven Analysis because:
      • Differential analysis is broken down into a ROI figure which can be compared to a standard of risk measurement.
      • Comparison of ROI to the hurdle is more straitforward than the evaluation of breakeven as a percentage of market, or margin of safety.

9.2 Differential Cash Flow

  • Differential cash flow analyzes costs and revenues that
    • are future-oriented
    • affect cash
    • are different between alternatives
    • are recurring

9.3 Differential Investment

  • Differential Investment
    • Differential investment analyzes expenditures that are
      • Future-oriented
      • Cash
      • Different between alternatives
      • One-time
    • Examples of Investments
      • Property, plant, or equipment
      • Intangible assets
      • Increase in working capital

  • Differential Working Capital Investment

    • Differential working capital investment refers to the amount of additional money that would be tied up in working capital.

    • Differential Working Capital

    • Differential Inventory

    • Differential Accounts Receivable

    • Differential Accounts Payable

9.4 Differential ROI and Payback

  • Differential ROI

    • Differential Return of investment (ROI) relates the size of investment to the recurring cash flows expected.

  • Differential Payback

    • Differential Payback is an alternative way of expressing the relationship between return and investment. It measures the length of time needed before an investment pays for itself.

  • When caclulating differential ROI or payback, do not include financing and related interest costs in the differential analysis.

    • Financing decisions are not made on a project by project basis. Firms seek to achieve a certain overall debt to equity ratio.
    • Differentials are concerned with only operating decisions.
  • Once ROI or Payback has been calculated, it should be compared to a company-specific hurdle rate.
    • Hurdle rate is the minimum target rate of return for the project specified in the differential analysis.
    • Hurdle rate is always higher than the bank rate since it contains a risk premium factor.

9.5 Differential Framework

  1. Outline alternatives
  2. Evaluate the qualitative factors involved in the decision.
  3. Identify the relevant items, those that are future-related, cash, and different.
  4. Separate recurring flows from one-time flows to do differential cash flow and differential investment.
  5. Calculate ROI/Payback
  6. Evaluate ROI against hurdle rate
    • If curdle rate is not given, evaluate the risk of the venture versus the return and make the call.
  7. Perform sensitivity analysis if necessary
    • Consider variables that are subject to significant uncertainty and that are large enough to potentially alter conclusions.
  8. Make a decison

10. Comprehensive Cases

10.1 Common Steps for Comprehensive Cases

  1. Understand the role, goals and constraints of the decision-maker.
    • Outline the qualitative and quantitative objectives.
    • Document any constraints and discuss how the potential obstacles can be managed. These constraints should be factored into decisions and recommendations.
  2. Clearly outline all necessary decision and recommendations to be made.
    • Read the case thoroughly for any "analysis triggers", like words "wondered", "wanted to", etc. to obtain additional decisions and recommendations to be made.
    • Understand the interconnectedness of decisons and recommendations.
      • What decisions depend on othes?
      • What decisions can be made independently of others?
      • Do any decisions require follow up recommendations.
      • Examples: if your decide to purchase the new equipment, where will you recommend the business obtain financing?

  3. Determine the type of analysis required to make each decision/recommendation.

    • Cost Behaviour Analysis
      • Analysis Types
        • Contribution Analysis
        • Breakeven Analysis
        • Sub-unit Analysis
        • Cost-price Analysis
      • Example Situations
        • New product launch
        • Pricing decision
        • Assess relative performance of existing sub-units to isolate poor performer.
    • Marketing Analysis
      • New product/service launch
      • New market expansion
      • Existing product/service evaluation
      • Assess fit within industry
      • Develop a new plan or revise current promotion plan
    • Cash Budget Analysis
      • Experiencing cash flow problems
    • Statement of Cash Flow Analysis
      • Cash management issues.
      • New Financing is required
      • Understand the current financial position of the company
    • Ratio Analysis
      • Anytime an Statement of Cash Flow is analyzed
      • Understand the past
      • Profitability assessment (vertical)
    • Differential Analysis
      • Assess a future opportunity
    • Projected Statement Analysis
      • Assess a future opportunity
      • Assess the overall strategy effectiveness
      • Determine amount of financing required

  4. Understand the organization.

    • Business Size-up
      • Types of analysis may include: industry/market, PEST, consumer, competitive and coporate capabitilies.
      • What factors are working for the company? What factors are agains.
    • Financial Size-up
      • Understand the past financial performance of the business
      • Types of analysis may include: statement of cash flows preparation, and interpretation, ratio preparation and interpretation, contribution and sub-unit
      • What doe this mean for futue opportunities? should changes be made?
  5. Assess opportunities, perform analysis to make decisions.
    • What are the qualitative considertations?
    • What are the quantitative results?
    • How does sensitivity analysis affect the results?
    • How do the goals and constraints influence your decisions?
  6. Evaluate overall strategy effectiveness.
    • Use projected statements to demonstrate the effectiveness of the decisions.
    • What do the results of the projected statements reveal?
      • Projected statement of earnings will reveal changes in profitability.
      • Projected statement of financial position will expose financing requirements.

10.2 Comprehensive Case Framework

  1. Understand the Organization
    • Business Size-Up
      • Industry Analysis
        • 3 facts and their implications
      • Consumer Analysis
        • If there is only one group of consumer, answer this:
          • Who are they?
          • When do they purchase?
          • Why they want the product?
          • What quanlities they want?
          • How to approach them?
        • If there are more than one groups of consumers, answer the above questions for each of them.
        • When answering questions, try to list a fact and an implication.
      • Competitive Analysis
        • For each competitor, answer the followings questions, and for each question, list a fact and an implication.
          • Who are they
          • Their relative size / market power
          • Advantages and Weaknesses
          • Industry Statistics
        • List out your own company's competitive advantages - how you can compete
      • Corporate Capabilities
        • 3 pros and their implications
        • 3 cons and their implications
    • Financial Size-Up
      • Statement of Cash Flows
        • If the case does not give a statement of cash flow, build one and then analyze. If it is given, then only make the analysis.
        • For analysis, answer from the following points and make an implication for each.
          • Operation
            • Is the company generating a positive cash flow from operation?
            • Does the use / source of AR and AP match? if not, we should remember to check the ages of AR and AP later.
          • Large Sources/Uses
            • What is the largest use or source?
            • What does that mean?
          • Financing Matching
            • How is the use of cash finance?
            • Long term finance should be for long term use, like use equity or debt to invest, not to pay off accounts payable.
          • Whether additional financing is needed?
            • Check the cash level and see how much is left, how mush is expected to be spent next year.
            • If needed, where to get the finance?
            • Mention to check the debt to equity ratio or interest coverage later.
      • Ratio Analysis
        • Find the ratios mentioned in the SCF analysis.
        • Calculate ratios for both years, and then compare the current year's ratio with the past and the industry.
      • Contribution Analysis (optional)
      • Segment Reporting (optional)
  2. Assess the Future Opportunities
    • Quanlitative
    • Quantitative
      • Possible Analysis
        • Differential Analysis (Always)
        • Contribution Analysis (optional)
        • Breakeven Analysis (done with contribution analysis)
        • Segment Reporting (done if required)
        • Cash Budgeting
      • Sensitivity Analysis (done with contribution on only done qualitatively)
  3. Make a Decision
    • Decision Part - which option to choose
      • Which option is chosen
      • Pros of the current option
      • Cons and of the current option. Then justify the cons.
      • Acknowledge other options, and list why not choos them
    • Finance Part - how to fince the chosen option
      • Which approach to take to finance the option - debt or equity?
      • Why that finance approach.
    • Other related questions if mentioned in the case.
  4. Evaluate the Effectiveness of Your Strategy
    • Action Plan and Contingency Plan
      • Short-Term Plan
        • Obtain financing
        • Implement the decision made
      • Long-Term Plan
        • Look forward to the future to come up with a 3 or 5 year plan
        • Be creative and practical.
      • Contingency Plan
        • List 3 risks and the mitigation plan for each
    • Projected Statements
      • Income Statement
      • Statement of Financial Position

10.3 How to Make a Good Comprehensive Case Analysis

  • Questions to Reflect
    • Has the appropriate analysis been performed?
      • Have the implications (conclusion) been drawn from the case facts?
      • Do the implications relate to the decisions and recommendations to be made?
    • Have all decisions and recommendations been made?
    • Are the decisions consistent with the analysis and are they supported by evidence?
    • Are the propose changes implementable?
    • Have the results of the decisions on the company as a whole been analyzed?
